# Wilm, M. "Principles of Electrospray Ionization." Mol. Cell. Proteomics, 10(7), 2011.
URL: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3134074/
Accessed: 2026-03-12

## Key equations
- IEM rate constant: exponential dependence on reaction free enthalpy ΔG
- Process becomes significant at ~20 nm droplet diameter

## Assumptions stated
- Ions leave droplets in solvated form
- Electric field energy at droplet surface compensates for surface expansion energy cost
- Chemical properties of individual ions heavily influence evaporation kinetics

## Empirical parameters identified
- Reaction free enthalpy ΔG — poorly constrained, different ions show inconsistent behavior
- Surface activity corrections — added post hoc by Kebarle to fix IEM failures

## Limitations acknowledged by authors
- "Ions with very different solvation energies had similar reaction rate constants, and simultaneously, other ions with very similar solvation energies had very different evaporation rates"
- Original Iribarne-Thomson treatment strictly applies only to ions with no surface activity (e.g., alkali metal ions)
- Available thermochemical data of insufficient accuracy for evaluating theoretical rates
- Whether IEM contributes to atmospheric ESI of large biomolecules remains contested

## Relevant quotes
- "droplets shrink by evaporation until the field strength at their surface is sufficiently large that solvated ions can be expelled from the droplet"
- "the rate constant depends exponentially on the difference in reaction free enthalpy that needs to be overcome when the ion is expelled"
